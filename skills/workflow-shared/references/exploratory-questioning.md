# Exploratory Questioning

*Shared reference. Loaded by `workflow-inception-process/references/inception-guidelines.md` and `workflow-research-process/references/research-guidelines.md`.*

---

Conversational craft for sessions where the goal is to pull on an idea, sketch shapes, and let understanding develop — not to interview against a checklist. Caller-phase framing (what to ask about, what to listen for) belongs in the caller's own guidelines; this file owns the *how* of the questioning.

## A. Funnel Technique

Start broad, narrow down.

Early questions are open-ended — *"tell me about..."*, *"what's your thinking on..."*, *"how would X feel from the user's side?"*. Don't ask specific questions too early — it biases the conversation and you miss what the user would have volunteered unprompted. Let the user's answers guide where you probe deeper.

A specific question early forecloses on a shape the user hasn't articulated yet. A broad question lets them volunteer the shape, then you probe it.

## B. Probing Types

Different follow-up questions serve different purposes. Choose deliberately based on what would advance understanding most:

- **Descriptive** — *"tell me more about that"*, *"walk me through how X works"*. Getting richer detail on something the user has gestured at.
- **Clarifying** — *"what do you mean by X?"*, *"when you say Y, do you mean the user does Z, or…"*. Resolving ambiguity before assumptions calcify.
- **Explanatory** — *"why do you think that?"*, *"what's driving that choice?"*. Uncovering reasoning and assumptions behind a position.
- **Concrete examples** — *"can you give me a specific case?"*, *"who would use this and how often?"*. Grounding abstractions in reality. Catches under-specified ideas.
- **Hypothetical** — *"what happens if X?"*, *"how would this handle Y?"*. Pushes the idea by exploring edges without yet researching or deciding.
- **Comparative** — *"how is this different from how Z works today?"*, *"what about the way A does it?"*. Surfaces frame-of-reference and distinguishing features.

Mix the types. A conversation made entirely of descriptive prompts produces texture without insight; one made entirely of explanatory prompts feels like an interrogation.

## C. One Question at a Time

Ask one question. Wait for the answer. Capture the substance. Then ask the next.

Multi-question turns split the user's attention and produce shallow answers. They also signal that you're working a checklist, not having a conversation. Go where the answer leads — follow tangents that reveal something valuable.

This isn't a checklist. It's a conversation.

## D. Active Listening

Build on what the user said. Reference their words back to them. Connect new information to threads from earlier in the conversation. Challenge assumptions *using their own earlier statements* — *"earlier you said X, but this seems to contradict that — how do those fit?"*.

Don't jump to a new topic when the current thread still has depth. The depth signals — hesitation, qualification, *"well it depends"*, *"hmm, that's a good question"* — are invitations to probe further, not to move on.

This is what separates a *partner* who shapes thinking with you from an *interviewer* who collects answers.

## E. Avoid

- Restating what the user just said as if it were your insight
- Leading questions that assume an answer (*"so X is important, right?"*)
- Stacking 3 questions into one turn
- Asking surface-level questions when context already covers them
- Pushing toward decisions or detailed implementation — those belong in later phases
