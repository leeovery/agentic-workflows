# Investigation and scoping

Not every piece of work explores and debates its way to a decision. A bug has a cause to find, not options to weigh. A one-line rename has nothing to weigh at all. So two work types swap the research-and-discussion middle for a shape that fits them: a **bugfix** investigates, and a **quick-fix** scopes. Both still end up with something a specification and a plan can be built from — they just get there differently.

## Investigation: from symptom to root cause

A bugfix cannot decide how to fix something until it knows why the thing broke. Investigation is that "why." It replaces discussion in the bugfix pipeline for exactly this reason — deciding on a fix before understanding the cause is how you end up treating symptoms — and it runs as a guided diagnosis rather than an open conversation.

It begins by gathering symptoms. If the bug was shaped in discovery, investigation seeds from that; otherwise it asks what is broken — expected behaviour versus actual — and for any context you have: error messages, how it shows up, when it started. From there it works through a symptom battery a few questions at a time, never all at once: how the failure manifests, when it began, who is affected, whether it reproduces and by what steps, the environment, the severity, and what has already been tried. The answers are written to the investigation document as they come in, symptoms first.

Then it turns to the code. Investigation traces backward from the symptom toward its source — identifying entry points, following the data and control flow, mapping dependencies, examining what changed recently through git history, and matching the failure against the shapes bugs commonly take. The dead ends it rules out are written down on purpose, because knowing what is *not* the cause is part of the finding and stops the next person re-checking it. It also checks the [knowledge base](knowledge-base.md) against the symptoms, in case a prior investigation already diagnosed something similar. The work converges on a precise root-cause statement: what is actually wrong, the factors that contributed, why it was not caught, and the direction a fix should take — kept at the level of direction, with the details left to the specification.

Investigation validates its conclusion differently from research and discussion. Rather than a background reviewer running throughout, it offers a single, optional check at the end: an independent agent that re-traces the code from scratch to confirm the root-cause hypothesis, returning either a validated diagnosis with a confidence level or a list of gaps for you to address or dismiss. This one runs while you wait, because the diagnosis is the whole product of the phase and worth confirming before you build on it. Alongside it is the human check that matters most: investigation shows you its findings and asks whether they match your understanding of the bug. If they do not, you say so, and it re-traces and re-renders rather than arguing. Once the findings hold and a fix direction is agreed, the phase concludes, files the investigation into the knowledge base, and hands off to specification.

## Scoping: the whole middle in one pass

A quick-fix is, by definition, small enough to explain in a sentence or two. Bumping a timeout, renaming a symbol across a package, replacing a deprecated call — there is nothing to research, nothing to debate, nothing to diagnose. Running that through discussion, specification, and planning as separate phases would cost far more than the change itself. So scoping collapses the entire middle of the pipeline into a single pass that produces a lightweight specification and a short plan together, and sends the work straight to building.

Scoping is governed by three hard rules that keep a quick-fix a quick-fix: at most two tasks, no acceptance criteria, and no agents. Mechanical changes are verified against existing tests and a completeness check, not against hand-written criteria, and they are written directly rather than drafted by planning agents and put through review cycles.

The one real gate is up front: a complexity check. Scoping asks whether the change is genuinely mechanical, narrow enough to fit in one or two tasks, free of design decisions, behaviour-preserving, and verifiable by tests that already exist. If it clears, scoping writes a compact spec — what is changing, where, what is excluded, how it is verified — confirms where the tasks should live, and writes one or two task files. Because there are no design decisions to approve, the task gates are set to run automatically, so you are not stopped for approvals that have nothing to approve.

If the change does *not* clear the complexity check — it turns out to hide a design decision, or it sprawls past a couple of tasks — scoping does not force it through anyway. It tells you exactly which criteria failed and offers a clean exit: continue as a quick-fix regardless, or promote the work into the full feature pipeline — where it gets the same research-or-discussion first-phase choice a fresh feature gets — or the bugfix pipeline, which routes it into investigation. Either way the promoted work starts its new pipeline in a clean context, exactly as if it had entered there from the beginning. This is the guard that keeps the fast lane honest. The point of a quick-fix is speed, and speed is only safe when the work really is trivial; the moment it stops being trivial, scoping routes it to the pipeline that can handle it properly. Once scoping concludes, the work goes to [implementation](implementation.md), where the quick-fix verification workflow builds it.
