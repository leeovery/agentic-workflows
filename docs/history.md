# History

This is the one page that speaks in the past tense. Everywhere else describes the system as it is; here is where it came from, because the shape of the thing makes more sense once you know which problems bent it into place.

It began in late November 2025 as a couple of markdown files. The very first taught Claude Code to write a planning document before writing code; within the hour it had been reshaped into a three-phase idea — discuss, then plan, then implement. That was the whole seed: write the thinking down, in order, before touching the code. Everything since has been that one conviction deepened and hardened — that documents drive the work, and the quality of the document chain sets a ceiling on the quality of the code. What changed across the months was not the conviction. It was who does the bookkeeping.

## The prose era

Through the winter of 2025 into early 2026, the family of phases filled out — review, specification, research joined discussion and planning — and all of it lived in prose. The skills described your project's state in sentences; Claude worked out where things stood by reading around the directories; every dashboard was hand-drawn text, re-derived from written instructions each time it was shown. Early in 2026 the pieces of the modern shape appeared: work split into distinct pipeline shapes behind a single entry point, cross-topic dependencies, specifications drawn from multiple sources, and installation through a proper command-line installer. The philosophy was all there. It was simply being carried, in its entirety, inside the model's head.

## State finds a home

The pivotal move came in March 2026, in what turned out to be the busiest month in the project's history. A project's state stopped living in narrated markdown and moved into a structured record with a real read-and-write surface. Skills no longer described progress in prose; they recorded it as data and read it back. Around the same time the inbox capture system landed, the project cut its first release as an installable product rather than a personal configuration, and — in April — it gained a memory: completed artifacts became a searchable index with provenance and confidence, so a phase could finally ask whether a question had been settled before.

May reworked the front of the funnel. What started as an "inception" idea became the universal first phase every piece of work now begins in, and for large initiatives it introduced the map of topics with routing and self-healing analysis. A week after it shipped it was renamed to what it is today: discovery. This was also when the system articulated its firmness gradient out loud — discovery is soft, discussion hardens the thinking, the specification is golden — the same gradient the phase chapters still describe.

## The bug that built the engine

The largest change of all started with a small rendering bug. Resuming a large epic, a couple of topics in the hand-drawn topic map wrapped past their margin and snapped the tree in half on screen. The immediate fix was trivial. The diagnosis was not: the skill prose had instructed the model to wrap each summary at one width *and* to prefix every line with a margin, so a line "short enough to never break" quietly became too long. Layout that was fully determined by the data was being reconstructed, by hand, from prose — and "wrong by default, correct only when watched closely" is not a rendering strategy.

A survey found the same pattern everywhere: dozens of places hand-drawing trees, hundreds of hand-counted separators and gates, menus matched back to routes by their labels. Over a few days in June the fix was reframed as the first piece of something much larger, with an economic argument at its centre. Handing the whole orchestration to code and reducing the model to an API call was ruled out — the model is the better driver — so the architecture inverted the other way: the model stays the driver, and code becomes the dependable thing it consults. From that decision came [the engine](engine.md), the strict separation between what the model reasons from and what you are shown, and the ruling that typed state belongs in the structured record while prose keeps the knowledge. Judgment decides, code records.

## The re-platforming

What followed, from mid-June through July 2026, was a long, stacked rebuild done in waves and reviewed continuously: the core's skeleton first, then the epic dashboard, then the transactions that change state, the start-up pipeline, the per-phase migrations, the machinery of the implementation loop, the reshaping operations, and finally the folding of the old state record wholly into the core. Each wave was proven against a live copy of a real project rather than in the abstract, and the endgame ran wave after wave of adversarial review — parallel fleets hunting for defects, a certification pass, a dedicated hunt for pre-existing bugs, a final re-review — until the record could be closed as complete and certified.

The monthly rhythm tells the same story in miniature: a few dozen commits at the start, into the hundreds as the phases filled out, a peak in the month state moved into structure, and a final concentrated push that was almost entirely the engine and its reviews. The through-line worth keeping is this: every era moved one more class of work from "the model re-derives it in prose" to "code answers, the model consults" — and at no point did the conversation stop being the driver. That balance, not any single subsystem, is what the system is.
