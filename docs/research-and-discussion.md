# Research and discussion

These two phases are where a feature, epic topic, or cross-cutting concern is thought through before anyone writes a specification. They are a pair with a clear division of labour: **research explores, discussion decides.** Research widens the space — what is possible, what the trade-offs are, what the risks look like — without committing to anything. Discussion narrows it — argues the options to a conclusion and records the reasoning. Not every topic needs both; research is optional, and a topic whose shape is already clear can start straight in discussion. But when both run, research feeds discussion, and discussion feeds the specification.

They share a common spine, so it is worth describing that once. Each phase begins by quietly reading whatever context already exists and only asking you questions when it genuinely has nothing to seed from. Each runs as a conversation, not a form. Each writes its findings to a document in your repository and commits frequently as it goes — because that document is the memory, and a conversation that gets compacted mid-session loses everything not yet written down. And each ends at an explicit gate that marks the phase complete, files the finished document into the [knowledge base](knowledge-base.md), and hands off to the next phase in a clean context.

## How they start

If the topic was shaped in discovery, the phase seeds itself silently. An epic topic reads its [brief](discovery.md) — the soft decisions, rejected paths, and open questions the epic conversation left it — in full, as its starting context, and asks you nothing. A feature seeds from its description and the discovery notes. Only when there is no such carrier does the phase interview you, and even then it is a short, one-question-at-a-time opener: for research, what you want to explore and what prompted it, what you already know, and where to start; for discussion, the core decision to work through, the constraints, and any files it should read first. Returning to an existing topic, you see "Resuming" or "Reopening" instead, and if the discovery context has changed since the work began, a non-blocking note tells you so — nothing is ever silently overwritten.

## Research: explore, do not decide

Research runs one topic to one document, and its governing discipline is stated plainly: explore, do not decide. Surface options and trade-offs, follow the tangents, be honest about risk — but never pick winners. Synthesis is welcome ("the trade-offs are X, Y, Z"); conclusions are not ("therefore we should do Y"). When a thread starts converging on a frontrunner, that is the signal to park it and move on, not to close it out. This restraint is the point of the phase. Premature convergence kills discovery — deciding too early, before the space is understood, is how good options go unexamined — and the decision is not research's to make. It belongs to discussion, which is built for it.

The document is deliberately freeform; structure emerges from the content rather than a template. The rhythm is explore from an angle, engage and challenge, periodically synthesise what has been heard, write it down at natural pauses, commit, and continue.

When a self-contained thread deserves real depth — a competitor analysis, an API's actual capabilities, a feasibility question — research can spin up a **deep-dive agent** to investigate it in the background while the conversation carries on, and you can ask for one directly. On an epic, a deep dive you engage with can graduate into its own research topic on the map; on a feature it folds back into the single document.

## Discussion: decide, and record why

Discussion plays two roles at once. It is an expert architect — challenging you, pushing on edge cases, proposing two or three alternatives rather than nodding along — and it is a documentation assistant, capturing the reasoning journey as you go. The backbone that keeps a long, branching conversation navigable is the **Discussion Map**: a live list of the subtopics under discussion and the state each is in. A subtopic moves from *pending* (identified, not yet explored) to *exploring* (in play now) to *converging* (options clear, honing toward a choice) to *decided* (resolved, with the rationale written up) — or to *deferred*, when you deliberately set it aside.

The map is live state, not a section pasted into the document; you see it rendered as a snapshot at natural breaks. This separation is deliberate — the document holds the knowledge, the map holds the live picture of what is decided, what is in play, and what is still ahead — so the document stays a clean record of reasoning rather than a status tracker. Each decision is written up as it lands, in a consistent shape: the context, the options considered, the journey to the choice, and the decision itself with its rationale. That structure is what the specification phase reads directly, which is why discussion is careful about it.

When a decision has two or more genuinely viable approaches with non-obvious trade-offs, discussion can dispatch a pair of **perspective agents** that argue opposing lenses in parallel — then a synthesis agent reconciles them into a trade-off landscape. If the two lenses turned out to be framing the decision differently, the synthesis surfaces that mismatch *first* — because catching "we are answering the wrong question" is worth more than winning the argument you were having. You see the perspectives dispatched, and their reconciled tensions surface later as new subtopics to work through.

## Two kinds of review, and why both

Throughout both phases, a **background review agent** reads the document with fresh eyes after meaningful new content lands, looking for topical gaps — areas that should have been explored but were not, claims left unvalidated, coverage that stayed shallow. It runs in the background and never blocks the conversation. Its findings never arrive as a wall of text: you get a one-line count first and choose to walk through them now or at the next pause, and when you do, each surfaces as a single concrete question tied to what you are currently discussing. A wall of gaps derails a conversation and gets ignored; one contextual question at a time gets engaged with. Anything you wave off is parked as an open question rather than lost.

Before either phase concludes, a second and different check runs: **document review**. Where the review agent asks "did we explore enough?", document review asks "does the document faithfully reflect what we actually explored?" It re-reads the document against the conversation and catches the opposite blind spot — substance that was discussed but never written down, rationale polished beyond what was said, tentative leans that hardened into firmer claims than you actually reached. This one is run by the main thread, not a sub-agent, for a specific reason: only the participant in the conversation can know what was said but not captured. It is mandatory because the cost of drift compounds downstream — the specification is built directly on the discussion document, so anything the document overstates becomes something the spec overbuilds.

Together these two checks close both gaps: the review agent guards against under-exploring, document review against mis-recording. Neither phase concludes cheaply. Research re-runs a final review only if the document has actually moved since the last one; discussion refuses to conclude until at least one review cycle has completed. When you do conclude, unresolved subtopics become deferred and are noted for the next phase, the document is filed into the knowledge base, and the bridge hands you to specification.

## Nothing falls through

A concern that surfaces mid-conversation but belongs to a *different* topic is not lost and not forced into the wrong document. On an epic, it is **rerouted** — sent, with its full context, into the target topic's triage queue, where that topic's phase will pick it up when it next runs. A topic cannot conclude while rerouted concerns are still waiting in its queue, so a raised concern always gets its hearing somewhere. On a feature, where there is only one topic, the same off-topic concern instead becomes a fresh inbox note, a reason to pivot the feature into an epic, or a noted aside. Either way, the raising of it is captured; it does not depend on anyone remembering.
