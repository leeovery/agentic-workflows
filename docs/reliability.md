# How it stays reliable

A system you hand months of work to has to be trustworthy in a specific way: it must not quietly drift. The status it shows you today should mean the same thing tomorrow; a decision captured last week should still be there next month; a session that crashes mid-task should not leave you guessing what actually happened. This reliability comes from a few guarantees you can feel, even though the machinery producing them stays out of sight. You do not manage any of it — you just do not experience the drift it prevents.

**There is one authority on the state of your work.** The assistant reasons, proposes, and talks with you, but it never decides on its own what the true state of your work is. Anything that can be worked out from what is already recorded is worked out once, the same way every time, and everything reads from that single source. So you will not get one answer today and a subtly different one tomorrow because the assistant recalled the situation loosely. Status is derived from your files, not remembered; a phase is complete because the record says so, not because someone said "done." State changes are consistent and repeatable rather than improvised — which is why phases cannot be skipped, and why a half-finished session resumes exactly where it stopped.

**Your work is written down as it happens.** The system treats finished thinking as something to be saved into version control continuously — at natural breaks, before context is refreshed, as each document completes — rather than left floating in a conversation that could be lost. Because the work lands in git along the way, your history is real and recoverable: you can see how a decision was reached, and nothing important lives only in a chat window. Kill a session whenever you like; the next one picks up from disk. The system's own memory is committed with the same discipline whenever it changes.

**The files stay current with the design.** As the system itself improves, the shape of older projects' records could fall out of step with how things work now. That gets reconciled quietly at start-up: existing work is brought forward to match the current design, safely and repeatably, so you are never operating on a stale or half-migrated project. This is invisible when nothing needs changing, and when something does, you are shown a plain-language summary of what moved before anything continues.

**Delivery is checked against what you agreed.** The final phase's whole job is to hold the built work up against the record that produced it — the discussion that shaped it, the specification that defined it, the plan that scheduled it — and confirm they match. "Done" is not a feeling anyone reports; it is a check against durable agreements you made earlier and approved.

Put together, these are the guarantees the system rests on: one source of truth for state, a history you can trust, files that never silently rot, and delivery measured against what you actually decided. Decisions are captured, the capture is authoritative, and the outcome is validated back against the capture. That closed loop is what lets you leave the loop running and trust what you come back to.
